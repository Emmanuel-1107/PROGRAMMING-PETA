PETRA
Petra, known in Arabic as ٱلْبَتْراء (Al-Batrāʾ) and in Greek as Πέτρα (Petra), originally referred to by its inhabitants as Raqmu or Raqēmō, stands as a significant historic and archaeological city nestled in southern Jordan. Renowned for its impressive rock-cut architecture and intricate water conduit system, Petra earned the moniker of the "Rose City" owing to the hues of the sandstone from which it was sculpted. Immortalized in a 1845 poem by John Burgon as "a rose-red city half as old as time," Petra lies adjacent to the majestic mountain of Jabal Al-Madbah, situated within a basin encircled by mountains delineating the eastern border of the Arabah valley stretching from the Dead Sea to the Gulf of Aqaba. Access to this remarkable city is facilitated by traversing the famed and picturesque 1.2-kilometer-long (3⁄4 mile) gorge known as the Siq, leading directly to the Khazneh.

The region surrounding Petra has been inhabited since as early as 7000 BC, with evidence suggesting that the Nabataeans possibly settled in the area by the 4th century BC, ultimately establishing it as the capital of their kingdom. Archaeological excavations have unearthed traces of Nabataean presence dating back to the second century BC, signifying Petra's pivotal role in the region's history. As nomadic Arabs, the Nabataeans capitalized on Petra's strategic location along incense trade routes, transforming it into a thriving commercial hub.

Their astute business ventures bolstered their prosperity, with Petra emerging as the epicenter of their wealth. Proficient in navigating the arid desert terrain, the Nabataeans adeptly utilized the mountainous landscape to fortify their city against adversaries. Their expertise in water harvesting, agriculture, and stone carving enabled them to thrive in this challenging environment. Petra reached its zenith in the 1st century AD, marked by the construction of Al-Khazneh, believed to be the mausoleum of Nabataean king Aretas IV, and a peak population estimated at 20,000 inhabitants. Employing an intricate network of cisterns, channels, and dams, they effectively harnessed rainwater, sustaining their flourishing civilization. While many renowned rock-cut structures, predominantly tombs, originate from this era and subsequent periods, fewer remnants remain of the city's freestanding edifices.
While the Nabataean kingdom became a vassal state of the Roman Empire during the first century BC, it wasn't until 106 AD that it forfeited its sovereignty entirely. Subsequently, Petra succumbed to Roman rule, leading to the annexation of Nabataea and its renaming as Arabia Petraea. The city's prominence waned with the emergence of maritime trade routes, compounded by the devastation caused by an earthquake in 363 that ravaged many of its structures. Despite the construction of several Christian churches during the Byzantine era, Petra's decline persisted, eventually resulting in its abandonment save for a few nomadic inhabitants by the early Islamic period. Its existence remained concealed from the Western world until 1812, when Swiss explorer Johann Ludwig Burckhardt chanced upon its rediscovery.

Designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985, Petra has garnered acclaim as "one of the most invaluable cultural assets of humanity." Recognized as one of the New 7 Wonders of the World in 2007, Petra stands as an emblem of Jordan and serves as the country's premier tourist destination. Tourist arrivals reached a zenith of 1.1 million in 2019, marking the first instance of figures surpassing the million mark. However, tourism in this historic city experienced a setback due to the COVID-19 pandemic, though it began to rebound shortly thereafter, with visitor numbers reaching 905,000 in 2022.

ROYAL TOMBS
The Royal Tombs of Petra are in the Nabatean version of Hellenistic architecture, but their facades have worn due to natural decay. One of these tombs, the Palace Tomb, is speculated to be the tomb for the kings of Petra. The Corinthian Tomb, which is right next to the Palace Tomb, has the same Hellenistic architecture featured on the Treasury. The two other Royal Tombs are the Silk Tomb and the Urn Tomb; the Silk Tomb does not stand out as much as the Urn Tomb. The Urn Tomb features a large yard in its front, and was turned into a church in 446 AD after the expansion of Christianity.

In 2016, archaeologists using satellite imagery and drones discovered a very large, previously unknown monumental structure whose beginnings were tentatively dated to about 150 BC, the time when the Nabataeans initiated their public building programme. It is located outside the main area of the city, at the foot of Jabal an-Nmayr and about 0.5 mi (0.8 km) south of the city centre, but is facing east, not towards the city, and has no visible relationship to it. The structure consists of a huge, 184 by 161 ft (56 by 49 m) platform, with a monumental staircase along its eastern side. The large platform enclosed a slightly smaller one, topped with a comparatively small building, 28 by 28 ft (8.5 by 8.5 m), which was facing east toward the staircase. The structure, second in size only to the Monastery complex, probably had a ceremonial function of which not even a speculative explanation has yet been offered by the researchers.
