COLOSSEUM

The Colosseum (/ˌkɒləˈsiːəm/ KOL-ə-SEE-əm; Italian: Colosseo [kolosˈsɛːo]) stands as an elliptical amphitheatre situated in the heart of Rome, Italy, positioned just east of the Roman Forum. Esteemed as the largest ancient amphitheatre ever constructed, it retains the title of the world's largest standing amphitheatre despite its antiquity. Construction commenced under the Emperor Vespasian (r. 69–79 AD) in 72 AD and concluded in AD 80 under his successor and heir, Titus (r. 79–81). Additional modifications were undertaken during the reign of Domitian (r. 81–96). These three emperors, patrons of the construction, collectively constitute the Flavian dynasty, hence the amphitheatre's designation as the Flavian Amphitheatre (Latin: Amphitheatrum Flavium; Italian: Anfiteatro Flavio) by subsequent classicists and archaeologists, owing to its association with the Flavius family name.

Constructed from travertine limestone, tuff (volcanic rock), and brick-faced concrete, the Colosseum boasted an impressive capacity, estimated to accommodate anywhere from 50,000 to 80,000 spectators at various junctures in its history, with an average audience size of approximately 65,000. Throughout its tenure, it served as the venue for a diverse array of events, including gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, executions, dramatic enactments of famous battles, and productions rooted in Roman mythology, including brief simulations of naval battles. However, its use for entertainment purposes ceased during the early medieval era. Over time, it found new roles, serving as a domicile, workshops, accommodation for religious orders, a fortress, a quarry, and eventually a Christian shrine.

NAME


Originally, the structure was simply referred to by its Latin name, amphitheatrum, meaning 'amphitheatre'. Although the modern designation Flavian Amphitheatre (Latin: Amphitheatrum Flavium) is commonly used, there is no evidence to suggest its usage during classical antiquity. This name highlights the patronage of the Flavian dynasty, under whose reigns the edifice was erected, yet it is more widely recognized as the Colosseum. In ancient times, Romans may have informally referred to the Colosseum as the Amphitheatrum Caesareum, with Caesareum being an adjective associated with the title Caesar. However, this name may have been purely poetic, as it was not exclusively applied to the Colosseum; Vespasian and Titus, the architects of the Colosseum, also constructed a Flavian Amphitheatre in Puteoli (modern Pozzuoli).

The term "Colosseum" is believed to have originated from a colossal statue of Nero, reminiscent of the Colossus of Rhodes, which Emperor Hadrian positioned adjacent to the amphitheatre. The term "Colosseum" stems from the Latin adjective "colosseus," signifying gigantic. By the year 1000 AD, "Colosseum" was commonly used to refer to the amphitheatre due to its proximity to the "Colossus Solis." Variations in spelling, such as "coloseum" and "coliseum," emerged in Medieval Latin. In the 12th century, it was referred to as "amphitheatrum colisei." During the High Middle Ages, it was known as "colosé" in Old French and "colisée" in Middle French. Middle English adopted "colisee," as evidenced by its usage by John Capgrave in the 15th century. Translations of Marcus Aurelius' biography mentioned "Collisee." The Italian term "colosseo" initially denoted the Roman amphitheatre and later any amphitheatre. By the 15th and 16th centuries, other languages, such as Catalan, Spanish, and Portuguese, adopted similar terms.

USE


The Colosseum served as the venue for a multitude of events, including gladiatorial shows and various other spectacles. Known as munera, these shows were organized by private individuals rather than the state, blending a strong religious component with demonstrations of power and familial prestige, captivating audiences with their immense popularity. Another highlight was the venatio, or animal hunt, which featured a diverse array of wild creatures, primarily sourced from Africa and the Middle East, ranging from rhinoceroses and hippopotamuses to elephants and giraffes, along with a myriad of other species such as aurochs, wisents, Barbary lions, panthers, leopards, bears, Caspian tigers, crocodiles, and ostriches. These battles and hunts unfolded amidst elaborate sets adorned with movable trees and buildings, often on a grand scale; for instance, Trajan reputedly commemorated his victories in Dacia in 107 with contests involving a staggering 11,000 animals and 10,000 gladiators spanning 123 days. Executions ad bestias, wherein the condemned were sent into the arena unarmed and naked to face ferocious beasts, were staged during lunch intervals, resulting in their gruesome demise. Additionally, acrobats and magicians would entertain the audience during these interludes.
During the early days of the Colosseum, ancient writers recorded that the building was used for naumachiae (more properly known as navalia proelia) or simulated sea battles. Accounts of the inaugural games held by Titus in AD 80 describe it being filled with water for a display of specially trained swimming horses and bulls. There is also an account of a re-enactment of a famous sea battle between the Corcyrean (Corfiot) Greeks and the Corinthians. This has been the subject of some debate among historians; although providing the water would not have been a problem, it is unclear how the arena could have been waterproofed, nor would there have been enough space in the arena for the warships to move around. It has been suggested that the reports either have the location wrong or that the Colosseum originally featured a wide floodable channel down its central axis (which would later have been replaced by the hypogeum).

Sylvae or recreations of natural scenes were also held in the arena. Painters, technicians and architects would construct a simulation of a forest with real trees and bushes planted in the arena's floor, and animals would then be introduced. Such scenes might be used simply to display a natural environment for the urban population, or could otherwise be used as the backdrop for hunts or dramas depicting episodes from mythology. They were also occasionally used for executions in which the hero of the story – played by a condemned person – was killed in one of various gruesome but mythologically authentic ways, such as being mauled by beasts or burned to death.
